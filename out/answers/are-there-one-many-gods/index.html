Are there one or many Gods? Do Deuteronomy 6:4, Isaiah 43:10, 44:6, 8 contradict Christian monotheism?

There is one God who exists in three persons

The argument

Some argue that the Bible is contradictory concerning whether there are one or three Gods. Here are the relevant passages:

There is only one God

Deuteronomy 6:4 - Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one.
Isaiah 43:10b - ...Before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me.
Isaiah 44:6 - ... besides me there is no god
Isaiah 44:8 - ... Is there a God besides me? There is no Rock; I know not any.

There are many Gods

Genesis 1:26 - Then God said, "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness..."
Genesis 3:22 - Then the Lord God said, "Behold, the man has become like one of us in knowing good and evil..."
Genesus 11:7 - Come, let us go down and there confuse their language, so that they may not understand one another's speech.
1 Corinthians 8:5 - For although there may be so-called gods in heaven or on earth—as indeed there are many "gods" and many "lords"...
1 John 5:8 - the Spirit and the water and the blood; and these three agree.

The answer

There is only one God

Foundationally, there is only one God, as Deuteronomy 6:4, Isaiah 43:10, Isaiah 44:6, and Isaiah 44:8 state.

Other "gods" are false gods that do not truly exist

When the Bible speaks of other "gods," it is speaking of false gods that do not exist, as demonstrated by the following passages:

Formerly, when you did not know God, you were enslaved to those that by nature are not gods. (Galatians 4:8)
...and have cast their gods into the fire. For they were no gods, but the work of men's hands, wood and stone. Therefore they were destroyed. (Isaiah 37:19)
Has a nation changed its gods, even though they are no gods? (Jeremiah 2:11a)

Possibilities concerning the Genesis passages

In Genesis, when it refers to God using the words, "us" and "our," there are two reasonable possibilities that do not contradict the doctrine that there is only one God.

  1. The plural pronouns could point towards the doctrine of the Trinity, which states that the one God exists in three "persons," or that God is one in substance and three in person, which is not a contradiction. There is a distinction between "substance" and "person."
  2. The plural pronouns could be a "royal we." Sometimes, the pronoun "we" is used to refer to a king or monarch.

God is one in substance and three in person

1 John 5:8 points towards the doctrine of the Trinity, which teaches that the one Godhead exists in three persons. In other words, there is a sense in which God is one, and there is another sense in which God is three.

God is one in the sense that He is one in substance. God is three in the sense that He exists in three "persons": 1) the Father, 2) the Son, and 3) the Holy Spirit.

The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are distinct in that each one is the "terminating front-person" for the various acts of God. In other words, while the entire Godhead performs all the actions of God (since God is one), the different things that God does is generally "attributed" to one of the persons of God. This is what we mean when we say that a particular person of God is the "terminating front-person" for a particular action of God.
