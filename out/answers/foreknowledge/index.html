Is predestination merely God's foreknowledge of future human decisions?

What the Bible really teaches about God's "foreknowledge"
Michael Cheng
Oct 2nd, 2018
...

The argument

Some argue that predestination in the Bible merely means that God predestined those whom he foreknew would freely choose to have faith in Jesus. In other words, rather than directly causing anyone to have faith, predestination simpy involves God looking down the corridor of time to see which humans would choose Jesus themselves.

The primary two passages used to support this view of predestination are Romans 8:29 and 1 Peter 1:1-2.

For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers.
...To those who are elect exiles of the Dispersion in Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia, according to the foreknowledge of God the Father...

The argument is that this passage teaches that God's predestination is based upon his "foreknowledge," which is merely passive knowledge of what humans would freely decide for themselves—predestination is not God actively deciding exactly whom He would save.

The response

The key to responding to this argument against the Calvinistic view of predestination is recognizing that this argument's definition of God's "foreknowledge" does not come from the Bible, but rather imposes an outside definition of "foreknowledge" upon the Bible. In the Bible, God's "foreknowledge" is not passive, but is actually active. Here is what Jeremiah 1:5 says concerning God's "foreknowledge" of Jeremiah:

Before I formed you in the womb I knew you, and before you were born I consecrated you; I appointed you a prophet to the nations.

In this passage, we learn that God "knew" Jeremiah "before" he was born, or He "foreknew" Jeremiah. However, here, God's "foreknowledge" of Jeremiah is not a foreknowledge of what kind of a person Jeremiah would be, but rather an active determining of the life that Jeremiah would live. Jeremiah did not choose himself to become a "prophet to the nations," but rather, God "foreknew" Jeremiah and determined that he would become a "prophet to the nations."

The difference between predestination and God's foreknowledge

Some object to this view of God's foreknowledge because they claim that this makes predestination and God's foreknowledge the same thing, whereas there is a clear distinction between the two in Romans 8.

However, this is a false claim because there is indeed a significant difference between predestination and God's foreknowledge. God's foreknowledge of a person involves God actively fore-loving a person (not passively waiting to see how that person would respond to Jesus in the future), and predestination refers to the specific plan that God has set in motion to save that person. They are both part of the Golden Chain in Romans 8. One leads to the other; God's foreknowledge leads to predestination.

Here are several passages that help us understand that God's "foreknowledge" of a person is an active fore-loving of that person, rather than a passive knowledge of what that person would do.

In the very same chapter as 1 Peter 1:1-2, we find a clarification concerning what God's "foreknowledge" is:

He was foreknown before the foundation of the world but was made manifest in the last times for the sake of you...

In this passage, we learn that Jesus was "foreknown" by God. However, this "foreknowledge" of Christ is clearly not just a passive knowledge about Christ, but is rather an active, intimate love of Christ.

Exodus 33:17 says this:

And the Lord said to Moses, "This very thing that you have spoken I will do, for you have found favor in my sight, and I know you by name."

Again, we see here that God's knowledge of Moses is not a mere passive knowledge, but rather involves love and intimacy. It implies a personal relationship with the one whom God "knows."

Amos 3:2 also helps us understand God's "knowing" something:

You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities.

Again, here, we see that God's "knowledge" of Israel is not passive at all, but involves a personal relationship with Israel. God does not just know about Israel; He knows Israel personally and intimately.

Conclusion

When we use the Bible to define God's "foreknowledge," we find that God's "foreknowledge" does not undermind the Calvinistic view of predestination at all, but rather enhances our understanding of predestination. God's "foreknowledge" and "knowledge" of a person involves a fore-loving of that person, as well as a personal and intimate relationship with that person. It is not passive at all, but rather leads to action on God's part, or a "predestining" of that person to a particular end.
